Engage Users Only as Often as Necessary

For one year, I participated in a fellowship program and served as a green infrastructure technologies researcher for a low income Syracuse neighborhood called the Near Westside. The Near Westside had become a sort of test lab for student research and projects. At the time it was the third most impoverished census block in the country, and it was devoid of street trees due to a wind blast storm in 1998. Geography, architecture, and land use planning students surveyed the blocks and administered survey after survey. I even assisted with one such survey. Many of the participants struggled with the survey because of their English abilities. Many grew maiais just seeing the number of questions. Others imparted a strong apathy for “yet another survey”.

My frustrations with this survey (and others) grew out of the apparent lack of empathy for the participants’ economic condition, immediate needs and concerns, and language abilities. In tandem to this, I was working on my green infrastructure research and realized that I could not add to the residents' survey fatigue. The Near Westside Initiative non-profit organization has a strong relationship with a cohort of civically engaged residents. I reached out and coordinated with this organization to form a committee of resident representatives who could impart the values and preferences of the community, and co-design the project with me.

This information was pivotal in my research, as I was able to narrow my list of (scientifically) viable green infrastructure implementation projects to a shorter list that would meet the beautification and safety needs of the residents. From there, I collaborated with another non profit (Home Headquarters) to find a vacant parcels that did not have future viability for housing construction, and we built two pocket parks.

The Qual Paints the Full Picture with the Quant

My understanding of the complexities of the Near Westside neighborhood would grow as my thesis advisor conducted research on their food security. I was loosely and tangentially involved with her project, but the learning lessons proved foundational to my current approach to qualitative research.

In 2009, I took a GIS course and in one particular lesson, graduates in the department presented their GIS-focused capstone projects. I found one project fascinating - and so did the University. The woman used GIS to locate all grocery stores and all stores selling fresh produce. She found that there were zero such stores within the Near Westside Neighborhood, and created a map showcasing this, and wrote her paper and conclusions based on strictly quantitative methods. The neighborhood was henceforth referred to as a food desert.

Jump ahead to 2012. I am working with my thesis advisor on formulating a thesis topic. She presents to me research that she is formulating, and invites me to work with her. Due to funding logistics, I was not able to work directly on this project, but I was able to stay current on the methods and results during our frequent check-ins on my thesis. Her project was in response to the aforementioned quantitative approach to understand the availability of fresh foods in the Near Westside. She took on a qualitative approach, and designed a survey to assess where and how the residents accessed grocery and fresh foods. The participants were recruited through phone screenings and were offered a $25 giftcard incentive for participating.

Her conclusions painted a completely different picture from what the quantitative study originally derived. Although there are no stores in the neighborhood, there is strong social cohesion. And she found that as long as one resident had a car, all residents had access to a ride to the grocery store. And most were even shopping at the Wegmans in an affluent neighborhood about 20 minutes away. The biggest need, she found, was not access to the stores, but access to cooking classes, as many did not know what to do with the fresh produce that the had available to them! This connection between the quantitative study and the qualitative study resonated and influenced my community planning work in that I made an effort to always interview the residents, to paint as full a picture as possible.
